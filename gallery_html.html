Gallery & Favorite Pieces

A curated collection of pieces that have stayed with me.

Every piece on this list found me at a specific moment — some during a late night study session, some through a film, some recommended by a friend. I've organized them loosely by genre, but honestly they all blur together. Good piano music doesn't really respect genre boundaries.

I've added short notes next to each piece explaining what makes it special to me. These aren't formal music analyses — just personal impressions. If you haven't heard something on this list, I'd encourage you to look it up. Most are on YouTube or Spotify within seconds.

The list will keep growing. Piano repertoire is essentially endless, and I'm always finding something new that stops me in my tracks. A strange chord progression, an unexpected key change, a melody so simple it feels like you already knew it. That's the magic of it — familiarity and surprise at the same time.

🎼 Classical

  1. Chopin — Nocturne in E-flat major, Op. 9 No. 2
    The piece that made me fall in love with piano. It's tender and melancholy without ever feeling overwrought. The ornamentation in the melody sounds like someone searching for the right words.
  2. Debussy — Clair de lune
    Somehow both delicate and enormous. The middle section swells in a way that genuinely surprises me every time, even after hundreds of listens.
  3. Bach — Goldberg Variations (Glenn Gould, 1981)
    Less about the notes and more about Gould himself — his humming, his tempo choices, the sense that every decision is deeply considered. A lifetime of listening material.
  4. Ravel — Pavane pour une infante défunte
    Slow, stately, and impossibly sad. The title translates to "Pavane for a Dead Princess" — it earns that weight without ever being theatrical about it.

🎷 Jazz & Impressionist

  • Bill Evans — Peace Piece
    A single left-hand drone for almost seven minutes while the right hand wanders freely. It shouldn't work as well as it does. It's one of the most meditative recordings I know.
  • Keith Jarrett — The Köln Concert (opening)
    Entirely improvised, performed on a badly out-of-tune piano. The constraints seem to free him completely. The opening theme is instantly recognizable and endlessly warm.
  • Oscar Peterson — Hymn to Freedom
    Joyful in a way that feels hard-won. Peterson's technique is staggering, but this piece is all heart. It moves forward like a deep breath.

🎬 Contemporary & Film

  • Yann Tiersen — Comptine d'un autre été
    From the Amélie soundtrack. Repetitive in the best possible way — each cycle feels slightly more worn-in and comfortable than the last, like a favorite sweater.
  • Ryuichi Sakamoto — Merry Christmas Mr. Lawrence
    Deceptively simple. The gaps between notes carry as much meaning as the notes themselves. Sakamoto's use of silence is unlike anyone else's.
  • Max Richter — On the Nature of Daylight (piano reduction)
    Originally for strings, but the piano version feels even more exposed and honest. It shows up in films constantly because it perfectly underscores quiet grief.
  • Ludovico Einaudi — Experience
    Minimalist and sweeping at the same time. I've listened to this on long train rides and it makes everything outside the window look cinematic.
